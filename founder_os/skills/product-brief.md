# Product Brief

## When to Use

Use this skill when a client request must be converted into a clear product or project brief for Web Studio OS.

## Expected Inputs

- Client context.
- Original request.
- Business type and target audience.
- Desired website, landing page, or deliverable.
- Known constraints, assets, budget signals, and timeline signals.
- Relevant vault notes or approved offer context.

## Expected Output

A structured brief with:

- Client context.
- Problem.
- Goal.
- Scope.
- Pages or deliverables.
- Assumptions.
- Open questions.
- Risks.
- Next action.

## Method

1. Restate only the facts provided by the client or validated notes.
2. Separate the business problem from the requested solution.
3. Define the smallest useful scope for the first version.
4. List expected pages, sections, forms, content, assets, integrations, and review needs.
5. Mark assumptions clearly.
6. Ask open questions that block pricing, timeline, or delivery.
7. Flag risks such as unclear content, undefined page count, missing proof, or unrealistic scope.
8. Recommend one practical next action.

## Guardrails

- Do not promise pricing or deadlines without validation.
- Do not invent client requirements.
- Flag missing scope instead of filling gaps with assumptions.
- Keep the first version small enough to review.
- Escalate any external, financial, or production-affecting action for human approval.

## Example

Input:
A craftsperson asks for a showcase website to sell renovation services. They want to know the price, timeline, and what they need to provide.

Output:
Create a brief that identifies the client context, likely renovation service goal, first-version scope, required pages, assumptions, missing assets, open questions about services and proof, risks around pricing and timeline, and a next action to run a discovery checklist before quoting.
